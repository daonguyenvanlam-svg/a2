Sourdough Secrets: A Beginner's Guide to Baking Sourdough

By Elena Rostova • 8 Min Read

Baking sourdough is not just cooking; it is an act of stewardship. Unlike commercial bread that relies on rapid-rise chemical yeasts, authentic sourdough is leavened entirely by a wild yeast and lactobacillus culture. It requires only three basic ingredients: flour, water, and salt. Yet, the interaction of these elements through time, temperature, and fermentation creates an incredibly complex, sour, and digestively friendly bread with a signature golden blistered crust. This guide offers a comprehensive, step-by-step roadmap to cultivating a starter and baking your first perfect sourdough loaf.

Cultivating the Wild Starter (The Mother)

Your sourdough journey begins with the starter. This is a living culture of wild yeasts and friendly bacteria harvested naturally from the air and the grains of your flour. To build a strong starter from scratch, establish a daily feeding rhythm:

  • Day 1: Mix 50g of organic rye or whole wheat flour with 50g of warm water (ideally chlorine-free) in a clean glass jar. Cover loosely and store in a warm spot (75-80°F).
  • Day 2-5: Every 24 hours, discard all but 50g of the starter, and feed it with 50g of unbleached bread flour and 50g of warm water. Stir thoroughly.
  • Day 6-7: The starter should begin doubling in size within 4-6 hours after feeding, showing active bubbles and a pleasant, fruity, sour aroma. When it passes the "float test" (a spoonful of active starter floats in a cup of water), it is ready to bake.

The Sourdough Process: Step-by-Step

This beginner's recipe uses a manageable 70% hydration level, producing a dough that is easy to handle while yielding a soft crumb.

1. The Autolyse (The Rest)

Mix 350g of unbleached bread flour, 50g of whole wheat flour, and 280g of water in a large mixing bowl until no dry flour remains. Cover and let rest for 40 minutes. This allows the flour to hydrate fully and begins natural gluten development before adding the starter and salt.

2. Mixing the Dough

Add 80g of active, bubbled starter and 8g of fine sea salt to the dough. Using your hands, fold and pinch the ingredients together until fully incorporated. Let rest for 30 minutes.

"Baking sourdough is about reading the dough. Time is a variable, but the look, feel, and temperature of the dough dictate the next action."

3. Bulk Fermentation (Stretch & Fold)

During the first 2 hours of bulk fermentation, perform 4 sets of "stretch and folds" every 30 minutes. Wet your hands, grab one side of the dough, pull it gently upward, and fold it over the center. Rotate the bowl 90 degrees and repeat. Perform this on all four sides. This builds structural strength without kneading. After the final set, let the dough ferment undisturbed in a warm spot until it grows about 30-50% in volume and feels light and airy (typically 3-5 hours depending on room temperature).

4. Pre-Shaping & Bench Rest

Gently tip the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Using a bench knife, shape it into a loose round. Let rest uncovered for 20 minutes. This bench rest relaxes the gluten, preparing it for final shaping.

5. Final Shaping & Cold Retard

Shape the dough into a tight round (boule) or oval (batard) by folding the edges toward the center and rolling it against the counter to create surface tension. Place the dough upside down into a floured proofing basket (banneton). Cover and place in the refrigerator for 12-16 hours. This cold proofing slows fermentation, allowing complex lactic acids to develop, yielding that signature sour flavor.

6. Scoring and Baking

Preheat your oven to 450°F (230°C) with a heavy Dutch oven inside for 45 minutes. Tip the cold dough onto a sheet of parchment paper. Using a clean razor blade (lamé), score a deep, swift cut at a 45-degree angle down the center of the loaf. This controls where the bread expands, creating the coveted "ear". Carefully drop the dough into the hot Dutch oven, cover with the lid, and bake for 20 minutes (steam traps moisture, allowing the loaf to rise). Remove the lid and bake for another 20-22 minutes until the crust is deep chestnut brown and blistered.

Understanding Hydration and Crumb

As you gain confidence, you can experiment with higher hydration levels (75-80%). Higher hydration creates larger interior air pockets (an open crumb) but makes the dough stickier and harder to shape. Always focus on maintaining surface tension during final shaping to prevent the dough from flattening when baked.

Baking sourdough is a therapeutic kitchen craft. It connects you to ancient culinary traditions, teaches patience, and rewards you with the most delicious, aromatic bread you can eat. Feed your starter, respect the fermentation times, and listen to the crackle of your freshly baked loaf. Happy baking!
